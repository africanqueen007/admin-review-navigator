Petition for Extended Work-from-Home Arrangements
In Response to the Escalating Fuel Price Crisis  ·  Updated 19 March 2026

We, the undersigned staff members of the Asian Development Bank (ADB), respectfully submit this petition requesting an immediate and time-bound expansion of flexible work-from-home (WFH) arrangements. We make this request in light of an unprecedented escalation in domestic fuel prices driven by the ongoing conflict involving Iran and the disruption of oil supply through the Strait of Hormuz — a geopolitical crisis that is inflicting direct and sustained financial hardship on ADB staff in the National Capital Region (NCR) and surrounding areas. ADB currently provides staff with two (2) WFH days per week. We request that this be extended by one additional day to a minimum of three (3) WFH days per week for the duration of the fuel price emergency.

I. The Fuel Price Situation

The Iran conflict, which began in late February 2026, has caused immediate and severe disruption to global oil markets. The Philippines imports approximately 90% of its petroleum supply and is among the most exposed economies in Asia to this shock. The impact on domestic pump prices has been swift and escalating:

Documented fuel price increases (Source: Department of Energy, Philippines; Philippine News Agency, 16–17 March 2026)
  • Gasoline has increased for 11 consecutive weeks since the start of 2026, with double-digit weekly hikes beginning the week of 10 March 2026.
  • For the week of 17–23 March 2026, the DOE confirmed increases of ₱12.90–₱16.60/L for gasoline, ₱20.40–₱23.90/L for diesel, and ₱6.90–₱8.90/L for kerosene.
  • Following these adjustments, pump prices stand at up to ₱91.60/L for gasoline, ₱114.90/L for diesel, and ₱143.79/L for kerosene.
  • Year-to-date net increases now stand at approximately ₱36.30/L for gasoline, ₱57.55/L for diesel, and ₱55.10/L for kerosene — the highest weekly movements in Philippine recorded history.
  • Energy Secretary Sharon Garin confirmed at a DOE press briefing on 16 March 2026 that domestic supply remains adequate through end-April, but that price volatility will continue as long as Middle East tensions persist.

These are not marginal fluctuations. They represent a structural and sustained increase in the cost of living that disproportionately affects staff whose compensation is denominated in Philippine pesos and whose daily commute costs cannot be absorbed by existing employer-provided allowances.

II. The Direct Financial Impact on Staff

The daily cost of commuting to ADB headquarters has escalated to historic levels. With gasoline now reaching ₱91.60 per liter as of 17 March 2026 and diesel exceeding ₱114.90 per liter, a private car user commuting 20 kilometers one-way now faces a fuel cost of approximately ₱366 per day in fuel alone — before tolls, parking, and vehicle wear. This is more than double what the same commute cost at the start of 2026. For staff relying on public transport, current fares impose a round-trip cost of ₱100–₱300 per day, with the Land Transportation Franchising and Regulatory Board having approved fare hikes of up to 19% for buses and jeepneys effective March 2026 to reflect higher diesel input costs.

Indicative daily commute cost savings from one WFH day (based on verified DOE fuel prices and LTFRB fare data, 17–19 March 2026)
  • Private car, 10 km one-way: fuel saving of approximately ₱183/day at ₱91.60/L and 10 km/L city efficiency, plus tolls, parking, and vehicle wear.
  • Private car, 20 km one-way: fuel saving of approximately ₱366/day, plus additional operating costs.
  • Private car, 30 km one-way: fuel saving of approximately ₱550/day, plus additional operating costs.
  • Public transport, 10 km one-way: full round-trip fare saving of ₱100/day.
  • Public transport, 20 km one-way: full round-trip fare saving of ₱200/day.
  • Public transport, 30 km one-way: full round-trip fare saving of ₱300/day.

Note: Fuel costs are now more than double what they were at the start of 2026. These figures will continue to rise as the week of 17–23 March 2026 hikes take full effect.

III. The Net Cost of Working from Home

Working from home carries a modest incremental electricity cost. Based on the current Meralco residential tariff of ₱13.8161/kWh (March 2026) and the measured power consumption of standard home office equipment, this cost is well-defined and quantifiably below the commute savings set out above:

Incremental home electricity cost for a standard 8-hour WFH workday (Source: Meralco tariff, March 2026; appliance wattage per DOE-accredited data)
  • Laptop (45W), external monitor (25W), and LED lighting (20W) for 8 hours: approximately ₱10 per day
  • Adding a ceiling or desk fan (60W × 8h): approximately ₱17 per day total
  • Adding a 1 HP inverter air conditioner (~500W average × 8h): approximately ₱72 per day total
  • Adding a 1 HP non-inverter air conditioner (745W × 8h): approximately ₱99 per day total

The internet router, which runs continuously regardless of work location, carries zero marginal WFH cost. Even in the highest-cost scenario — non-inverter air conditioning running for the full workday — the incremental electricity cost of ₱99 remains well below the commute savings at all distance brackets above 5 kilometers, yielding a clear and positive net financial benefit for the staff member in all realistic scenarios.

At current prices, the net financial benefit of a single additional WFH day per week now ranges from ₱84 to over ₱1,600 per day depending on commute mode, distance, and home cooling arrangements. Each successive weekly fuel price increase widens this gap further.

IV. Policy Context and Precedent

Philippine government precedent: On 8 March 2026, President Ferdinand R. Marcos Jr. announced a four-day on-site workweek for select government offices, effective 9 March 2026, explicitly citing the impact of rising fuel costs on workers as the direct rationale. The host government has therefore recognized reduced on-site days as a legitimate and proportionate mitigation measure in response to the identical crisis now affecting ADB staff.

ADB peers in the multilateral development bank (MDB) community have similarly adopted or maintained flexible WFH policies as a standard feature of staff welfare provisions. The ability to mitigate extraordinary cost-of-living shocks through flexible work arrangements falls squarely within the spirit of ADB’s Staff Regulations and its obligations to maintain equitable and supportive working conditions. ADB’s own experience with hybrid work over the past three years has demonstrated that operational effectiveness is fully maintained under expanded WFH arrangements.

V. Our Request

The undersigned staff respectfully request the following:
  • Immediate extension of the current WFH arrangement by one additional day, to a minimum of three (3) WFH days per week for all eligible staff categories at HQ, for the duration of the fuel price emergency;
  • Establishment of a clear and transparent review mechanism, linked to objective DOE-published fuel price benchmarks, that triggers automatic reassessment of this arrangement as market conditions evolve;
  • Engagement of the Staff Council in good faith consultations on any adjustments to WFH policy, consistent with ADB’s commitment to staff participation in matters affecting working conditions; and
  • Communication to all staff of the criteria and timeline for any such review, to provide certainty and planning confidence during this period of economic volatility.

We make this request as a practical, proportionate, and data-grounded response to a verifiable and ongoing financial hardship — one that is reducing the real take-home value of staff compensation with each passing week and that lies entirely beyond the control of the staff affected. We are confident that ADB, as an institution that champions inclusive and people-centered policies in its development work, will respond with the same care toward its own staff.

Respectfully submitted by the undersigned ADB staff members.
